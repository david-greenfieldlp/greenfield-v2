<h2 id="">In the Beginning, There Was Hardware</h2><p id="">Before software ate the world, hardware built it. Apple, Intel, Cisco were not just tech companies; they were the early blueprints of venture capital success. The first wave of Silicon Valley was, quite literally, silicon.</p><p id="">And yet, for the past two decades, venture has pivoted hard towards software. The logic was simple: scalable, capital-efficient, high-margin businesses that move at internet speed. Hardware, on the other hand, slipped out of the spotlight—code was king, atoms were a liability. </p><p id="">Of course, software-driven models reshaped the landscape and will continue to dominate many categories, but with AI, the playing field resets daily.</p><p id="">Open-source models, fine-tuning toolkits, and cloud APIs have democratized access. What used to be a moat of technical complexity and model IP is increasingly just a starting line.</p><p id="">While software can still build massive businesses, in some of the most defensible AI plays, hardware integration is becoming a powerful amplifier of differentiation.</p><h3 id="">Today, Real Defensibility Lies in the Fusion of Hardware and Software</h3><p id="">AI software is evolving at breakneck speed. But with that speed comes a problem: it commoditizes fast. What’s cutting-edge one month can be yesterday’s demo the next.</p><p id="">This is where hardware steps back into the spotlight. In AI-native systems, hardware isn’t just the container, it’s the source of differentiation. The ability to generate, process, and act on unique data at the edge creates a feedback loop that software alone can’t replicate.</p><p id="">And when hardware and software are co-designed, deeply integrated, and infused with learning capabilities, something truly powerful happens. The system itself becomes intelligent, adaptive, and extremely difficult to replicate.</p><h3 id="">Exodigo: A Case Study in Full-Stack AI Moats</h3><p id="">At Greenfield Partners, we’ve seen this firsthand through our investment in <a href="https://www.greenfield-growth.com/our-companies#Exodigo" target="_blank" id="">Exodigo</a>. The company combines cutting-edge multi-sensor hardware with proprietary AI models to map the underground world—utilities, pipes, voids—with unparalleled precision.</p><p id="">Here’s the critical insight: the hardware itself isn’t just a delivery mechanism; it’s the source of proprietary data. Without Exodigo’s custom sensor stack, the AI has nothing unique to learn from; the sensor data is just noise. Only by owning and integrating both do you unlock the system’s full power.</p><p id="">This isn’t just a novel approach, it’s a defensible one. Every scan improves the model. Every deployment tightens the feedback loop. The result is a data moat that’s nearly impossible to replicate without matching the entire stack, something no one else in the space has done.</p><h3 id="">The Deep Tech Surge: Hardware-Heavy, Impact-Heavy</h3><p id="">It’s not just Exodigo. Some of the most consequential companies in today’s private markets are deeply hardware-driven:</p><ul id=""><li id="">SpaceX – $350B valuation, vertically integrated from rockets to satellites.</li><li id="">Anduril – $28B valuation, blending AI with defence hardware and autonomy.</li><li id="">The Boring Company, Helsing, Figure AI, Covariant, Agility Robotics — all combining physical systems with intelligence.</li></ul><p id="">These companies were once seen as outliers. Now they’re blueprints for how bits and atoms reinforce one another.</p><h3 id="">VCs Are Catching Up</h3><p id="">Firms like Valor Equity Partners and Lux Capital have long recognized this trend. They’ve consistently backed deep tech founders building complex systems that combine physics, software, and machine intelligence.</p><p id="">At Greenfield, we share this conviction, backing companies like <a href="https://www.greenfield-growth.com/our-companies#Exodigo" target="_blank" id="">Exodigo</a>, as well as innovators in photonics (<a href="https://www.greenfield-growth.com/our-companies#Dustphotonics" target="_blank" id="">DustPhotonics</a>), autonomous systems, as well as two unannounced investments in this space, and beyond. However, our lens isn’t limited to only hardware-heavy models. Whether it’s pure software or full-stack, what matters is defensibility: moats built from data, distribution, network effects, or, when the use case demands it, the integration of atoms and bits.</p><h3 id="">The Future Belongs to Full-Stack Builders</h3><p id="">To deep tech founders: you’re not swimming upstream anymore. Investors now understand that moats aren’t just made of code—they’re built from integrated systems that learn and improve together.</p><p id="">For some, that means full-stack architectures where hardware and software reinforce one another. For others, it will mean software-driven moats built on proprietary datasets, vertical workflows, distribution advantages, or network effects. The common thread is defensibility.</p><p id="">‍</p><p id="">---</p><p id=""><em id="">If you’re building in this space, whether through bits alone or the fusion of bits and atoms, we’d love to hear from you. </em></p><p id=""><em id="">Submissions are open for the AI Disruptors 60, our list recognizing early and growth-stage startups shaping the future of AI.</em></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><div class="button-container">
