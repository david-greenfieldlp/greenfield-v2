These macro factors, which are also affected by the geopolitical situations in the world, are dramatically affecting technology companies and startups. While we do not specialize in the macro climate, we wanted to provide the ecosystem with a summary of the key macroeconomic metrics that are rapidly changing. We believe that investors and founders should be aware of these changes and apply first principles to try and understand the effects it might have on their businesses.</p><p id="">‍</p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8;'><li>As a reminder, we recently published in our <a href='https://www.greenfield-growth.com/blog-posts/q2-2022-in-review' target='_blank'>Q2inReview report</a>, a compilation of all capital raises within Israel’s tech ecosystem, insights into the state of the venture market, and started seeing some slowdown in the Israeli venture market, which is correlated to the macroeconomics effects that we will discuss in this report</li><li>Inflation remained high last month (July 2022) at 8.5%, after peaking at 9.1% in June<ul><li>The 9.1% peak represents a 40+ year high</li></ul></li><li>The Fed funds effective rate is 2.33%, and forward markets anticipate a rise to 3.40% by the end of the year. In the recent Jackson Hole meeting, the Fed has announced that they are planning to keep playing on the aggressive side to do whatever it takes to bring down inflation to their 2% YoY target</li><li>Narrow technical recession has set in given negative GDP growth in Q2 2022 of (0.9)%, following a (1.6)% drop in Q1 2022<ul><li>Atlanta Fed (GDPNow) is predicting 1.4% growth in GDP for Q3 2022</li></ul></li><li>The US housing market has not yet been broadly affected, but is most likely on a correction course due to the rise in interest rates and stagnant economy; we could expect a decline in housing pricing (in some areas in the US)</li><li>Brent crude is still relatively elevated, but at ~$100 is ~18% off its recent high of $122</li><li>Consumer Confidence Index (CCI) and Purchasing Managers Index (PMI) are on the decline, both are indicators to the growth and strength of the economy</li><li>PMI is hitting 2 years low, but remains above 50 and still above pre-COVID levels</li><li>PMI is hitting 2 years low, but remains above 50 and still above pre-COVID levels</li><li>Unemployment remains at an all-time low territory at 3.6%, but participation rate has fallen off slightly for the first time since Covid</li><li>Bond yields have spiked since the beginning of 2022; however, they have started to decline in recent weeks</li></ul></div><p id="">‍</p><h3 id="">Inflation and Interest Rates Over Time</h3><figure id="" class="w-richtext-figure-type-image w-richtext-align-fullwidth" style="max-width:2131.5px" data-rt-type="image" data-rt-align="fullwidth" data-rt-max-width="2131.5px"><a href="https://uploads-ssl.webflow.com/5fec652174e94d7f707cd737/631494f0e68f955890eb84a0_Inflation%20and%20Interest%20rates%202.jpg" target="_blank" id=""><div id=""><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/66fa660f46b01edefb1e5fa6/6706a2b1e37278f989e9199f_6706935460f0ab3e13590a90_63148f4a99eb553fb2aae776_Inflation%252520and%252520Interest%252520rates.jpeg" width="auto" height="auto" alt="" loading="auto" id=""></div></a><figcaption id=""><em id="">Click to expand</em></figcaption></figure><h4 id=""><strong id="">Trend Explanations and Implications Moving Forward</strong></h4><p id=""><strong id="">Upward Inflationary Pressure</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8;'><li><strong>Drivers of inflation:</strong> sharply rising labor costs, factory shutdowns, global shipping backlog, and pent-up consumer demand</li><li><strong>Today:</strong> consumer prices have climbed more than 8% over the past year – fastest rate of increase in 40 years<ul><li>With demand outpacing supply, inflation will continue to erode the purchasing power of consumers</li><li>Vehicle prices up ~7%, food prices up ~10%, and energy prices up ~42% since last year</li></ul></li></ul></div><p id=""><strong id="">Monetary Policy Response</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8;'><li>The Fed is tightening monetary policy to fight inflation. Specifically, it is raising the short-term interest rate that it controls directly (aka the Fed funds rate)</li><li>Central bank officials use this rate to slow down the economy – in this case to weaken demand so that supply can catch up	<ul>	<li>    Most recently, the Fed raised interest rates another <strong>0.75%</strong> to about <strong>2.25%</strong> to <strong>2.50%</strong>.    </li>    <li>    In addition, the fed communicated that they would remain aggressive with interest rate raise in order to bringdown inflation to their 2% yearly target    </li>   	</ul></li><li>Because higher interest rates mean higher borrowing costs, people will eventually start spending less. Ideally, the demand for goods and services will drop and cause inflation to slowdown</li><li>By increasing the federal funds rate, the Fed is effectively trying to shrink the supply of money available for making purchases</li></ul></div><p id=""><strong id="">Looking Ahead</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8; background-color: powderblue;'><li>The benchmark interest rate will likely end the year around 3.4% and around 3.8% in 2023</li><li>The Fed is seeking to raise interest rates enough to curb inflation without causing a recession—a so-called <strong>soft landing</strong>	<ul>	<li>    The risk is that demand falls too much, which could lead companies to cut production and layoff employees, pushing the economy into a recession    </li></ul></li></ul></div><p id="">‍</p><h3 id="">Real GDP&nbsp;Overview</h3><p id="">‍</p><figure id="" class="w-richtext-figure-type-image w-richtext-align-fullwidth" style="max-width:4315px" data-rt-type="image" data-rt-align="fullwidth" data-rt-max-width="4315px"><a href="https://uploads-ssl.webflow.com/5fec652174e94d7f707cd737/631496975b8f6c1e8bc16497_Real%20GDP%20Overview.jpg" target="_blank" id=""><div id=""><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/66fa660f46b01edefb1e5fa6/6706a2b1e37278f989e919a2_6706935460f0ab3e13590a8d_631496975b8f6c1e8bc16497_Real%252520GDP%252520Overview.jpeg" width="auto" height="auto" alt="" loading="auto" id=""></div></a><figcaption id=""><em id="">Click to expand</em></figcaption></figure><h4 id=""><strong id="">Trend Explanations and Implications Moving Forward</strong></h4><p id=""><strong id="">Cooling Economic Growth</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8;'><li>The upward growth trajectory in the U.S. was disrupted when real GDP fell unexpectedly by <strong>1.6%</strong> in the first quarter of 2022 and most recently <strong>0.9%</strong> in Q2. This occurred after a nearly 7% gain during the previous quarter, prompting forecasters to lower growth projections for 2022 and 2023</li><li>Factors that have contributed to declines in GDP: sizable decreases in real net exports and inventory investment ▶ combined to subtract 4 percentage points from real GDP growth</li><li>On the bright side, personal consumption expenditures and nonresidential (business) and residential fixed investment combined to account for over 3 percentage points of real growth</li></ul></div><p id=""><strong id="">Looking Ahead</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8; background-color: powderblue;'><li>Economic growth seems to continue to cool throughout 2022<ul><li>Some of the reasons for the last two quarters decline in Real GDP Growth include high inflation and rapid monetary tightening, which curb consumption and business investment growth</li></ul></li><li>The GDPNow model estimate for real GDP growth (seasonally adjusted annual rate) in the third quarter of 2022 is 1.4%</li></ul></div><p id="">‍</p><h3 id="">Housing Market Trends</h3><figure id="" class="w-richtext-figure-type-image w-richtext-align-fullwidth" style="max-width:4266px" data-rt-type="image" data-rt-align="fullwidth" data-rt-max-width="4266px"><a href="https://uploads-ssl.webflow.com/5fec652174e94d7f707cd737/63149ac5b450d5918e158aa8_Housing%20Market%20Trends.jpg" target="_blank" id=""><div id=""><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/66fa660f46b01edefb1e5fa6/6706a2b1e37278f989e9198b_6706935460f0ab3e13590a96_63149ac5b450d5918e158aa8_Housing%252520Market%252520Trends.jpeg" width="auto" height="auto" alt="" loading="auto" id=""></div></a><figcaption id=""><em id="">Click to expand</em></figcaption></figure><h4 id=""><strong id="">Trend Explanations and Implications Moving Forward</strong></h4><p id=""><strong id="">Housing Prices Since ‘08 Crisis</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8; '><li>The housing market has generally recovered. Although prices fell <strong>33%</strong> during the Great Recession, they have rebounded and are now up more than <strong>50%</strong> since hitting the bottom of the market</li><li>The housing market is entering a new phase: homes are more available, buyers may be more scarce, and certain sellers may not want to put their houses on the market</li><li>Home prices have soared largely due to inflation, low supply, and pandemic-fueled demand over the past two years</li><li>The median selling price for existing homes stands on $403,800 after reached a record high of $413,800 in June</li></ul></div><p id=""><strong id="">Recently Falling Starts</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8; '><li>Housing starts in the U.S. dropped <strong>7.25% month-over-month</strong> to an annualized rate of <strong>1.446 million units</strong> in July 2022, the lowest figure since February of last year</li><li>The housing sector has been cooling due to soaring prices and mortgage rates</li><li>Single-family housing starts sank 8.1% to 982,000 while starts for units in buildings with five units or more came in at 568,000</li><li>Sustained declines in housing starts often serve as a sign that the economy is slowing down and a recession may be imminent</li></ul></div><p id=""><strong id="">Looking Ahead</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8; background-color: powderblue;'><li>The nation’s housing market might be on a correction course:	<ul>    <li>    Rising interest rates and a stagnant economy are already diminishing the rapid home-price appreciation the housing market has experienced over the past year</li><li>Several economists predict the deceleration in home prices will continue as homebuyer demand wanes</li></ul></li><li>As the Federal Reserve continues to take aggressive actions to curb inflation, mortgage rates will likely rise further, crushing affordability and curbing demand within the housing market. The average cost of a gallon of gas down almost a dollar since the June peak</li><li>Gas prices have been declining for several reasons, including an increase in gasoline production and a decrease in the cost of crude oil. Recession fears have also knocked down prices</li></ul></div><p id=""><strong id="">Looking Ahead</strong></p><div data-rt-embed-type='true'><ul style='line-height: 1.8; background-color:powderblue;'><li>High oil and gas prices are likely here to stay</li><ul><li>The West’s sanctions against Russia will yield insufficient alternatives, given that Russia accounted for 14% of global oil supply last year</li><li>Although soaring inflation and sluggish growth raise the likelihood of recession, global demand for oil is unlikely to fall enough to dent prices, as it did in 2008</li><li>As the Chinese government unwinds COVID-19 restrictions, pent up demand could push up prices.
